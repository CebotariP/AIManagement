AI in Governments

Artficial Intelligence can contribute personalized services and automated processes for the government. Some governments have begun to regulate AI and write policies about its implementation. Governments can harness AI by utilizing personalizes services and automated processes.

Singapore’s “Ask Jamie” is a virtual assistant that helps citizens and businesses navigate government services across roughly 70 government agencies through AI-powered chat and voice. Singapore additionally provides LearnAI, a program with customizable courses targeted at certain areas of society, such as students, industry professionals, educators, and the general public.
Singapore's AI research grants provide up to USD$718,000 to fund research on advanced AI.
Singapore's program, 100 Experiments (100E), aims to solve AI problems for actors across different sectors by providing USD$180,000 of funding per 100E project and giving access to a team of specialists.
Singapore is also launching 5 programs in areas throughout their country, such as smart cities and smart urban-mobility intitiatives for businesses to access government services and resources, along with an electronic payments project.
~ McKinsey

October 2017: In hopes to diversify their economy away from oil and modernize their economy through technology, the United Arab Emirates launched the UAE Strategy for Artificial Intelligence, one of the first of its kind in the gulf region.
~ McKinsey

October 2022: Saudi Arabia launched its National Strategy for Data and AI with the help of Saudi Data and AI Authority in Saudi Arabia to create a more data-driven economy.
~ McKinsey

The United States of America created the National Science Foundation, which founded multiple National Artificial Intelligence Research institutes which focus on AI themes. The US government announced $1 billion in grant awards for the creation of 12 new AI and quantum information R&D institutes.
~ McKinsey

2021: The European Union (EU) became the first governmental body in the world to comprehensively draft AI development regulations. They suggested that organizations should be enforcing these regulations when managing Artificial Intelligence.
~ McKinsey

2018: Finland launched Elements of AI, a free online course that would introduce AI basics to the public. The Finnish government pledged to educate at least 1% of it's population, which was a goal quickly met and furthermore surpassed. The course is now available in 20 languages and educates 750,000 people around the world.
~ McKinsey

The United Kingdom Research and Innovation committed to providing £530 million of funding for AI research and development, comprising £129 million for novel AI algorithms, tools, and techniques and £401 million for AI applications and implications.
~ McKinsey

AI in Healthcare

Artifical Intelligence has been used for both predictive healthcare and preventative care, such as robots that could do work that would otherwise cause long-term health issues to the people doing it.

The National Health Service in the United Kingdom set up a National COVID-19 Chest Imaging Database containing a shared library of chest X-rays, CT scans, and MRI images to support the testing and development of AI technologies to treat COVID-19 and a variety of other health conditions.
~ McKinsey

AI in Businesses

Artifical Intelligence has been able to help businesses increase productivity, efficiency, and manufacturing. Business leaders see AI as an opportunity.

The Centre of Excellence for Testing & Research of Autonomous Vehicles (CETRAN) and Nanyang Technological University in Singapore work together in collaboration to work towards AI innovations in autonomous vehicles by creating sandbox environments that replicate Singapore's roads with traffic schemes, road infrastructure, and traffic rules.
~ McKinsey

Taiwan's Ministry of Economic Affairs launched the Unmanned Vehicles Technology Innovative Experimentation Program, which allowed a company to conduct a one year experimental sandbox operation for Winbus , an electric, self-driving minibus.
~ McKinsey

AI in Economies

Artificial Intelligence can contribute to the overall global economic output. There are fears that AI can take away jobs, exploit, and abuse the economy.

Some early research suggests that AI could potentially deliver an additional global economic output of about $13 trillion by 2030, boosting global GDP by about 1.2 percent a year.
~ McKinsey

For the Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC), AI could lead to a potential combined value of $150 billion for their Economies and could add 6% + of each economy's GDP. This would, in turn, help them shift away from fossil fuels.
~ McKinsey

Fear-Mongering in Artificial Intelligence

Reflection of Personal Worldviews

All of the following fears stem from a reflection of our fears regarding human nature - thus, these fears surrounding AI are no longer unique to AI. Rather, these fears are just general fears projected onto AI.

The the nightmare AI scenario with the coldest of utilitarian logic and context-free, bloodless “logic” actually display the valued held by so many young tech-savvy men and Silicon Valley utopians alike and are seen as the ideal consciousness.
“Privileged people who fear an AI rebellion always imagine it in exploitative terms that mirror their own ideologies. They fear their ethics being turned back on them.”
~ Document Journal

Job Industry Threatened

Fear: By 2025, 85 million jobs may be displaced by a shift in labor division between humans and machines.

Fact: Even more roles will open up as a result of this shift - 97 million jobs to be exact. These are "jobs of the future" and can include even better opportunities, especially for early-career professionals.

~ World Economic Forum Report 2020

Fear of Better than Human Intelligence

Fear: People fear that AI may soon exceed human intelligence.
~ Analytics India Magazine

Fact: "Computers are incredibly powerful but they are also pretty dumb, and I think we need to work hard to make them fulfill the potential that they have and so that means teaching them to be smarter. Technology should augment the human intellect not replace it. It should be a powerful tool to help us think better, and I think that is really the journey we are on."
~ John Giannandrea (Senior VP of Engineering, Google)

Fear of an AI "Overlord"

Fear: The nightmare AI apocalyptic scenario where a machine operates on the coldest of utilitarian logic and end up killing humanity with context-free, bloodless "logic".
~ Document Journal

Fact:
The fears surrounding “an eventual AI ‘overlord’ ” are mostly exaggerated and dystopian in nature.
~ IBM Watson’s Senior Vice President David Kenny
"Why would a deity AI-like superintelligence be bound to an inflexible, rote, unempathetic logic that led it to alter history with dictatorial brutality, an “if-then” equation gone feral? When put in these terms, it looks less like a virtual god and much more like a sadly familiar, all-too-human mortal."
~ Document Journal
